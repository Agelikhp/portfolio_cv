Role/Contribution: Participating Artist - Cocurator.
Participating Artists: Angeliki Papageorgiou, Magia Papakosta & Sotiria Smirnaiou.
part of the "Athens 01" Project organized by weareATH
Athens, Greece June - July 2017

Description: As co-curator and artist for this collaborative studio space event, I showcased the talents of indigenous Athenian artists, aiming to highlight local creativity while fostering meaningful artistic dialogue within the community. At that time, I was deeply intrigued by how humans function, particularly the brain's role as the human "hard drive." This fascination informed my artistic approach, resulting in an amusing commentary on the human condition. The event not only celebrated diversity but also invited attendees to reflect on who "The Others" are in a city rich with cultural narratives.
My contribution included a video art projection featuring a 3D representation of human heads, accompanied by an experimental soundscape produced by using the platform "SuperCollider" for audio synthesis and algorithmic composition, that explored the complex musical behavior of audio feedback through the conceptualization and creation of an audiovisual art projection. It examined the interference and interactions among several feedback loops, utilizing audio feedback to create a unique musical ambiance that resonated with the themes of human experience and emotional complexity.

Process: I collaborated closely with fellow participants to curate their works while transforming an old house in the Kypseli area into a vibrant exhibition space from the ground up. My own artwork incorporated elements designed to encourage viewer participation and engagement, enhancing the overall experience.

Outcome: The event was met with enthusiasm, generating insightful discussions about local art practices and community identity. Attendees valued the opportunity to engage directly with artists, fostering a sense of connection and collaboration.

Here are some images that inspired me, by an old sideshow attraction called "The Headless Woman," which gained popularity in the 1930s and 40s. This macabre display was originally introduced to the USA by a German man, known as "Doctor" Egon Heineman. The act featured a woman with a headless illusion, where tubes and devices appeared to emerge from her neck, creating a chilling yet fascinating spectacle. Included are snapshots from my video project and a 3D sculpture based on a real sculpture I was developing during my research on the human head. My work not only reflects my interest in the eerie allure of such illusions but also examines the feminist aspects of "The Headless Woman." By exploring themes of identity and existence, I aim to highlight the complexities of female representation and agency within this historical context.
